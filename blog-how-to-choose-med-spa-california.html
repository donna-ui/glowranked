In California, med spas exist in a regulatory gray zone. They’re not hospitals, so they don’t face the same scrutiny. But they perform medical procedures â injections, laser resurfacing, chemical peels â that carry real risk when done wrong. Here’s how to evaluate any med spa before you book.

Step 1: Confirm There’s a Medical Director

California law requires that any practice performing medical procedures operate under the supervision of a licensed physician. The Medical Director doesn’t have to be on-site for every appointment, but they must be overseeing the practice’s medical protocols.

Ask directly: "Who is your Medical Director and are they licensed with the California Medical Board?" You can verify any physician’s license yourself at mbc.ca.gov. If a practice is evasive about this question, walk away.

Step 2: Verify Your Injector’s License

In California, injections must be performed by a Registered Nurse (RN), Nurse Practitioner (NP), Physician Assistant (PA), or physician. An esthetician â no matter how experienced â is not legally permitted to perform Botox or filler injections.

This distinction matters enormously. RNs and NPs are trained in anatomy, pharmacology, and adverse event management. An unlicensed injector who hits an artery during a filler treatment may not know how to respond. Complications from vascular occlusion can cause tissue death or vision loss.

Verify your injector’s RN license at rn.ca.gov. NP licenses are verified through the same site.

Step 3: Look for Real Before/After Photos â Not Just Stock

Before and after photos are the most honest signal of a provider’s skill. Look for:

  • Consistent lighting and angles (a sign of professional documentation)
  • Results that match the treatment â lip filler should look natural, not overfilled
  • Patient diversity â results across different skin tones and face shapes
  • Photos on their own website, not just curated Instagram grids

Be skeptical of practices that only show "best of" results or use stock images in their marketing materials.

Step 4: Ask About Their Adverse Event Protocol

A legitimate med spa will have a written protocol for handling adverse events â allergic reactions, vascular occlusion, burns from laser treatments. They should be able to tell you where the nearest emergency department is and whether they carry hyaluronidase (the enzyme used to dissolve hyaluronic acid fillers in case of vascular emergency).

If the staff seems confused by this question, that’s your answer.

Google reviews are useful, but look beyond the star rating. Read the one- and two-star reviews carefully. Are there patterns? Complaints about results going wrong and the practice being unresponsive? That’s more telling than a handful of five-star testimonials from the same week.

Also note how the business responds to negative reviews. A defensive or dismissive response to a legitimate complaint is a red flag.
